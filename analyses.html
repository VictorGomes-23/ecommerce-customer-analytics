RFM Customer Segmentation

Business Challenge

The company needed to identify high-value customers and optimize marketing spend across different customer groups. Without segmentation, all customers received the same treatment regardless of their value or behavior.

Methodology

  • Recency: Days since last purchase (more recent = better)
  • Frequency: Number of transactions (more frequent = better)
  • Monetary: Total amount spent (higher spend = better)
  • Scoring: Quintile-based approach (1-5 scale for each dimension)
  • Segmentation: Combined scores to create 5 distinct customer groups

Key Findings

Champions

512 customers (12%)

£3.6M revenue • £7,031 avg spend

Recent, frequent buyers with high spend. Best customers driving 41% of revenue.

Loyal Customers

1,247 customers (29%)

£2.8M revenue • £2,244 avg spend

Regular buyers with moderate spend. Solid revenue contributors.

Potential

867 customers (20%)

£1.4M revenue • £1,615 avg spend

Recent buyers with growth potential. Target for upselling.

At Risk

1,183 customers (27%)

£1.1M revenue • £930 avg spend

Previously active, now declining. Need retention campaigns.

Lost

563 customers (13%)

£127K revenue • £226 avg spend

Inactive customers. Win-back opportunity.

Business Impact

Pareto Principle Confirmed

Top 12% of customers (Champions) generate 41% of revenue

VIP Program Opportunity

Target 512 Champions with exclusive benefits (estimated £180K additional annual revenue)

Win-Back Campaign

563 Lost customers represent £127K recovery potential

Recommendations

  1. Launch VIP loyalty program for Champions with exclusive perks and early access
  2. Implement automated email sequences to upgrade Potential customers to Loyal status
  3. Create urgent retention campaigns for At Risk segment with personalized offers
  4. Design win-back campaign for Lost customers with special "we miss you" incentives
  5. Adjust marketing budget allocation to reflect segment value (60% to Champions/Loyal)

Churn Prediction Model

Business Challenge

Customer retention costs 5-7x less than acquisition. The company needed to identify customers likely to churn before they leave, enabling proactive retention campaigns rather than reactive win-back efforts.

Methodology

  • Model Type: Logistic Regression (interpretable, production-ready)
  • Target Variable: Churned = no purchase in 180 days after prediction point
  • Temporal Validation: Features from historical period, labels from future observation period (no data leakage)
  • Features (18 total): Recency, frequency, monetary, purchase trends, engagement patterns
  • Train/Test Split: 70/30 with stratification on churn status

03

Cohort Retention Analysis

Business Challenge

Understanding customer retention patterns over time is critical for forecasting revenue, optimizing customer acquisition costs, and identifying the most valuable acquisition channels.

Methodology

  • Cohort Definition: Customers grouped by first purchase month
  • Tracking Period: 13 monthly cohorts (Dec 2010 - Dec 2011)
  • Retention Metric: % of cohort making a purchase each subsequent month
  • Visualization: Heatmap showing retention rates by cohort and month

Key Findings

Month 1 Retention

37% average

Critical drop-off point. First 30 days determine long-term engagement.

Month 6 Stabilization

22% average

Retention stabilizes after 6 months. Customers who stay this long become loyal.

Best Cohort

Dec 2010 (45%)

Holiday shoppers showed highest month-1 retention. Strong seasonal effect.

Business Impact

£890K Annual Impact from 10% Improvement

Increasing month-1 retention from 37% to 47% adds significant lifetime value

Critical First Month Window

Identified 30-day window where retention interventions are most effective

Recommendations

  1. Implement automated welcome email sequence for new customers (days 1, 3, 7, 14, 30)
  2. Offer first-repeat-purchase incentive (15% off second order within 30 days)
  3. Increase holiday season acquisition budget to capture high-retention cohorts
  4. Create "month 1 survival kit" with product recommendations and usage tips
  5. Track cohort retention as key performance metric in executive dashboards

Key Metrics

Cohorts Analyzed 13
Avg M1 Retention 37%
Avg M6 Retention 22%
Best Cohort Dec 2010

A/B Testing Framework

Test Scenario

Tested two email marketing approaches targeting high and medium churn risk customers:

Control (A)

Standard Email

  • 10% discount offer
  • Generic messaging
  • Cost: £2 per customer

Treatment (B)

Personalized Email

  • 15% discount offer
  • Free shipping (£15 value)
  • Personalized product recommendations
  • Cost: £7 per customer

Statistical Results

Control Conversion

12.3%

1,000 customers

Treatment Conversion

18.7%

1,000 customers

Winner

Lift

+52%

Relative improvement

Business Impact

£47K Additional Annual Revenue

Full rollout to 2,846 eligible customers generates significant incremental profit

3.3x Incremental ROI

Treatment generates £3.30 for every £1 of additional investment

Statistically Significant (p < 0.001)

High confidence in results with proper experimental design

Recommendations

  1. Roll out Treatment (personalized email) to all 2,846 eligible high/medium risk customers
  2. Continue A/B testing with additional variants (20% discount, bundle offers)
  3. Apply personalization learnings to other marketing channels
  4. Monitor actual vs projected performance for 90 days post-rollout
  5. Use A/B testing framework as standard for all new campaigns

Test Parameters

Sample Size 2,000
Duration 14 days
P-value < 0.001
Statistical Power 80%
